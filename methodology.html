Data & Methodology

What every layer in the Luxembourg Rental Explorer is actually built from, how each score is calculated, and — just as important — what isn't real data. Nothing here is official government output; treat every number as a starting point for your own research, not a verdict.

01Boundaries

Country view uses simplified commune polygons (originally OpenStreetMap-derived, via a public GeoJSON mirror), dissolved and merged where communes have since amalgamated so the map matches the current 100 official communes. These are simplified for size and speed, not survey-accurate.

City view (Luxembourg City's 24 quartiers) does not use the official VDL quartier polygons — that data exists on the city's ArcGIS server but wasn't retrievable through the tools available while building this. Instead, quartier areas are a Voronoi tessellation: a diagram built from each quartier's real center point, divided so every point on the map belongs to whichever quartier center is nearest, then clipped to the real city outline. It tiles the whole city with no gaps, and areas roughly track real quartier sizes, but the actual boundary lines are a geometric approximation, not the legal ones.

02Affordability

Real price data, two sources depending on zoom level:

  • Country view: apartment asking prices (2021) or, where too few sales existed, house sale prices (2020), sourced from STATEC / Observatoire de l'Habitat notarial-deed data, via a cleaned dataset published on GitHub (b-rodrigues/rap4all). Where a commune had neither, it falls back to its canton's average — flagged in the detail panel as (canton avg. fallback).
  • City view: Immotop.lu quarterly market reports for Luxembourg City's 24 quartiers, 2023–2025 (mixed vintages — some figures are more recent than others, and smaller quartiers show real quarter-to-quarter volatility since they have few transactions).

Scoring

Linear min-max normalization, inverted so the cheapest area scores 100 and the most expensive scores 0:

score = 100 × (max_price − this_price) / (max_price − min_price)

Country and city views are normalized separately against their own min/max. A score of 60 in one view and 60 in the other do not represent the same price — they occupy the same relative position on two different scales.
Known drift: commune-level prices are 2020–21 figures. Nationally, prices have moved roughly −10% to +5% since, depending on the year — treat this layer as a relative ranking between areas, not current absolute prices.

03Connectivity

Real public-transport service level, from Luxembourg's national GTFS feed (the official schedule data published on data.public.lu). For a representative weekday — Wednesday 22 July 2026 — every scheduled departure was counted at every stop, across all modes: RGTR national bus, AVL city bus, TICE (south-region bus), Luxtram, and CFL rail. That's roughly 360,000 departures nationally across ~2,600 served stops. Each stop was then assigned to its commune (or quartier) by point-in-polygon against the same boundary layers the map draws, and departures summed per area.

Scoring

Total departures/day is log-scaled (it spans from a few dozen in the smallest communes to ~110,000 in Luxembourg City), min-max normalized, and blended 65/35 with a straight-line-distance-to-capital score. Frequency dominates; distance keeps a genuinely central location ahead of a far-flung town that happens to have dense local buses. Quartiers use the same method (75/25, distance-to-centre), normalized within the city.

This replaces the previous rail-only model (2022 CFL station ridership blended with distance). The effect is large and intended: heavily-bused suburbs with no train station — Niederanven, Strassen, Sandweiler — rise from the bottom of the layer to mid-pack, while small rural communes whose only signal was a lightly-used rail halt (a few hundred trains a day) fall to match their real service level. Each area's detail panel now shows its actual departures/day, served-stop count, and the bus / tram / rail split.

The map's transit overlay now draws the real route geometry for all three modes, as three independently-toggleable layers — bus (RGTR/AVL/TICE), tram (Luxtram), and rail (CFL). Each route's shape comes straight from the GTFS feed, Douglas-Peucker-simplified and de-duplicated into a shared network of polylines to keep the file small (the raw geometry is ~57 MB; the shipped network is a fraction of that). Lines that run off the edge of the country toward Trier, Thionville or Arlon are real cross-border services, not drawing errors. Rail also draws station markers, each plotted at its real GTFS coordinate — so the marker lands directly on the rail line — and sized log-scale by the number of scheduled trains/day serving that station (same feed and representative weekday as the geometry). A few cross-border and branch halts with no service on the sample summer weekday (e.g. Volmerange-les-Mines, Dudelange-Usines) are shown at minimum size and labelled as such.

Vintage caveat: the current GTFS feed (valid 14 Jul – 23 Aug 2026) covers the summer-holiday period, so school-only bus lines aren't running and some routes are on reduced summer schedules. This understates term-time service in a few rural communes where school buses are a large share of the timetable. Re-running against a school-term feed would lift those areas somewhat; the relative picture between well- and poorly-served areas holds either way.

04Amenities

A near-complete census of ~7,700 points of interest pulled from OpenStreetMap (via the Overpass API) — every shop, eatery, and public facility OSM knows about in Luxembourg. This replaces the previous model, which only sampled the ~35 largest communes via Google Places and filled the other two-thirds of the country with a weak population-density estimate. Every POI is placed into its true commune or quartier by point-in-polygon against the real boundary polygons, then sorted into nine categories:

Groceries & food (supermarkets, convenience, bakeries, butchers…), Restaurants / cafés / bars, Retail shops (all other storefronts), Healthcare (clinics, doctors, dentists), Pharmacies, Schools / childcare, Banks / ATMs, Gas stations, and Parks / sports. Each category is separately toggleable as a point layer on the map.

The score is a weighted blend of the log-scaled count in each category (groceries and dining/health weighted highest, fuel and leisure lowest), min-max normalised within each layer so 100 is the best-served area and 0 the least. Because it runs on real counts, amenity-dense towns like Bertrange (its retail count now reflects the La Belle Étoile mall) rank correctly, and thin rural communes rank low on fact rather than a density guess.

One deliberate filter: OSM tags thousands of private gardens and backyard swimming pools as leisure. Those are excluded — the parks/sports category counts only real public facilities (parks, sports centres, fitness centres, stadiums). OSM coverage in Luxembourg is very good but community-maintained, so a brand-new shop may lag reality by a little.

05Safety / Reputation

Luxembourg publishes no crime statistics below the national level. We checked directly: the Police Grand-Ducale's annual "chiffres de la délinquance" and the full 148-page activity report give country-wide totals only, plus an organisational map of 4 police regions with no crime figures attached; the national open-data portal entry is just a link to that PDF. There is no per-region, per-canton, or per-commune crime dataset to map. Luxembourg is also consistently ranked among the safest countries in the world.

So rather than fabricate a crime rate for 124 areas, this layer inverts the question: every commune and quartier is presumed generally safe (baseline 90/100), and an area is only marked down where a specific problem is documented in official sources or the press. A low score therefore means "there is a known, reported problem here," not a precise ranking.

Documented markdowns from a 2024–25 review of government sources and Luxembourg press:

  • Luxembourg City — Gare quartier (22) — the country's most-documented problem area and the focus of the government's official "Drogendësch 2.0" action plan (May 2025): open drug dealing, homelessness and prostitution concentrated around Rue de Strasbourg and Rue Joseph Junck; the Gare/Hollerich commissariat was made 24/7.
  • Hollerich (45) and North / South Bonnevoie (48) — the rest of that same enforcement zone; the government reports 35% of all city police patrols concentrate in Gare/Hollerich/Bonnevoie.
  • Commune of Luxembourg (68) — hosts those hotspots but is large and mostly safe (Belair, Limpertsberg, Kirchberg…), so the commune-wide markdown is mild.
  • Esch-sur-Alzette (58) — documented reputation for insecurity (press, 2025) with drug trafficking concentrated in the Brill quarter and around Rue du Canal / Avenue de la Gare.
  • Ettelbruck (72) — its commissariat was converted to 24/7 under Drogendësch (2025) over documented drug-related concerns; problems far more contained than the capital or Esch.
Everywhere else stays at the safe baseline. This makes the layer deliberately near-flat — that reflects reality — and it errs toward calling an area safe rather than inventing suspicion. It is a documented-incident model, not a measured crime rate.

06Combined score

The four layers are combined as a weighted average, with weights you control via the sliders in the map:

combined = (afford×w1 + connect×w2 + amenity×w3 + safety×w4) / (w1+w2+w3+w4)

Default weights are 35 / 30 / 20 / 15 (affordability, connectivity, amenities, safety) — a starting point, not a recommendation. Move them to reflect what actually matters to you; the map, top-picks list, and every score on it update live.

08Raw data

The underlying files this tool loads are in /data in the repository, if you want to inspect, verify, or reuse them directly:

  • communes.json — per-commune scores, prices, rail, and amenity data
  • communes.geojson — commune boundary polygons
  • quartiers.json — per-quartier scores and data
  • quartiers_voronoi.geojson — quartier Voronoi polygons
Built as a personal research tool, not an official product of the Luxembourg government or any of the organizations whose data it references. No warranty as to accuracy — verify anything decision-critical directly with the primary sources linked above.
